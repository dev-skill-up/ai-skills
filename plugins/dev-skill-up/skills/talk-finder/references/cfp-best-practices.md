# Writing CFP answers that get accepted

A program committee skims many proposals fast. The abstract has to prove three things quickly: you understand who's in the seats, you know exactly what you'll cover, and you're the right person to cover it. Optimize for demonstrating that competence, not for intrigue or cleverness.

## What reviewers are actually deciding

They are choosing, from many proposals, the ones most likely to deliver value to *their* audience in the available time. Every part of your answer should help them say yes to that. The most common framing error is answering "why is this speaker impressive?" when the first question in the reviewer's mind is "what does my audience get?" Lead with audience value; establish credibility second.

## The fields

### Title

Specific and intriguing, but never vague or cute at the expense of clarity. A reviewer (and later an attendee picking sessions) should know roughly what they'll get. Prefer a concrete promise or tension over a pun that hides the subject.

### Abstract (public-facing)

This is what attendees read when choosing sessions, and what reviewers weigh most. A strong shape:

1. Open with the problem or pain — the situation the audience recognizes.
2. Name the specific challenge or tension.
3. Hint at your resolution or point of view (don't give the whole talk away, but show there *is* one).
4. State who it's for and what they'll walk away with.

Write in **"you" language**: "You'll learn how to X" beats "I'll explain what I learned about X." Keep the audience, not the speaker, as the subject of the sentences.

### Longer description / notes to organizers

Often a private field reviewers see but attendees don't. Use it to remove doubt:

- **Scope and structure** — what's in, what's deliberately out, and roughly how the time is spent. Vague scope ("how to solve all your testing problems") makes reviewers doubt the talk can be delivered in the slot. Showing what you'll *cut* signals you can actually fit the slot.
- **Why you** — the specific experience, perspective, or insight that makes you the right person. Not a résumé; the relevant angle.
- **Format and any demos**, if applicable.

### Audience takeaways

The highest-leverage field, and the one most proposals fumble. Make them concrete and action-oriented: things an attendee can *do differently* afterward. "Understand best practices" and "gain insights" are non-answers — they give the reviewer no reason to pick you. Instead: "Write a custom lint rule that catches X," "Decide when to reach for Y vs. Z," "Run this on your own codebase Monday morning."

## Top reasons proposals get rejected

- **No audience focus.** Reads as if copy-pasted to sixty conferences, with no sign the speaker considered who's in the room.
- **Vague and passive.** Promises "insights" and "best practices" without specifying what, exactly. Gives reviewers no reason to choose it over a sharper proposal.
- **No speaker differentiation.** Says what the topic is but not why this person should present it.
- **Too niche.** Relevant only to a handful of specialists; loses to broader-appeal topics. (Fix by framing the niche thing as a case study for a general lesson.)
- **Unclear scope.** Tries to cover everything; reviewers can't tell how deep it goes or whether it fits the time.
- **Carelessness.** Misspelled technologies and typos read as lack of effort and are often enough on their own to justify rejection. Proofread, especially product and tool names.
- **Treated as a rough sketch** rather than a compelling, structured argument.

## Process

Write a long version first, then trim hard to the most important parts — editing down beats padding up. Tailor every submission to the specific conference and its tracks. Proofread for technical spelling. If time allows, the single best improvement is outside feedback, since it's easy to be tunnel-visioned on your own abstract.
