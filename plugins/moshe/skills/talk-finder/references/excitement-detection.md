# Detecting the live thread

The goal is to find the topic the person is genuinely energized by, because energy is what carries a talk from "accepted" through "well-prepared" to "memorable." You are reading text in a live conversation, so ignore advice about vocal pitch, pace, and facial expression — those don't reach you. What reaches you is the structure of their words. Read that.

## The core idea: read against their own baseline

There is no universal "enthusiastic writing style." A terse person's three-sentence answer can carry more heat than a verbose person's three paragraphs. So calibrate every signal against *that person's* baseline across the conversation, not against an absolute standard. Their first couple of answers establish the baseline; deviations from it are the signal.

## Positive signals (raise a topic's score)

**Spontaneous return.** They come back to a topic you didn't ask about, or steer an unrelated question back toward it. This is the single most reliable signal — it's hard to fake and survives differences in writing style. Weight it highest.

**Unprompted tangents.** They go down a side road — a related detail, a war story, a "this reminds me of" — without being asked. Tangents mean the topic is generating more thought than the question requested.

**Density of concrete detail.** Specific names, numbers, edge cases, "the annoying thing was," a particular failure they hit. Concreteness signals lived engagement; vagueness signals a topic they think they *should* care about but don't.

**Elaboration relative to baseline.** The answer runs noticeably longer or more energetically than their other answers. (Relative — see baseline above.)

**Evaluative and affective language.** "I love that," "the satisfying part," "it drives me crazy that," "what's beautiful here." Frustration counts — irritation at a hard problem is a form of engagement, often a strong one.

**Forward motion.** They talk about what they want to try next, what they'd build, unanswered questions they're chasing. Live topics point forward, not just backward.

## Negative signals (lower a topic's score)

**Performed enthusiasm.** Generic excitement words with no specifics behind them — "I'm really passionate about scalability!" with nothing concrete following. Adjectives doing the work that detail should. Score this *down*, not up; it often marks a topic they think is the "right" answer.

**Résumé framing.** Described in terms of credentials or impressiveness ("I led the team that...") rather than the ideas themselves. Signals they want to be *seen* talking about it more than they want to talk about it.

**Should-language and obligation.** "I probably should talk about X," "everyone keeps telling me to present Y," "it's the most relevant to my job." External pull, not internal pull.

**Flatness / quick closure.** Short, complete, and *done* — they answer and stop, no spillover, no return. Note that for a terse person this is just baseline; what matters is flatness relative to how they talk about other things.

**Defaulting to the prestigious or recent.** The biggest project, the newest tool, the thing they just shipped — chosen because it's salient, not because it's alive. Check whether the energy is real or just recency.

## Watch for inversion

The signal can flip, especially for the topic that matters *most*:

- Some people get **tersest** about what they care about most, because it feels too big to open or too personal to gush about. A clipped answer followed by a quiet return later is a strong tell.
- Some people **elaborate on everything** at length, which flattens word-count as a signal. For them, lean harder on spontaneous return, concreteness, and forward motion.

When raw verbosity and the other signals disagree, trust spontaneous return and concrete detail over word count.

## How to score

1. List every candidate topic from their answers, including the quiet ones. Contrast only surfaces the live one if the dull ones are on the page too.
2. For each, enumerate the specific signals present — quote or point to the actual words, don't just assert "seemed excited."
3. Weight: spontaneous return > concrete detail / forward motion > elaboration / affective language > raw length. Subtract for the negative signals above.
4. Allow an explicit null. A topic can score zero. "No strong signal on any of these yet" is an honest, useful read — say it and keep probing rather than inventing a winner.

This is a ranking to *steer* the live interview, not a verdict. Re-rank after every answer, dig into the current leader, and switch lines of questioning when everything stays flat.
